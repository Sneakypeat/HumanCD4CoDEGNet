# Video script — plain-spoken version (< 3 min)

*Short sentences. Every period is a breath. No jargon names to trip on.*

**[0:00 — Intro]**

Hi. I'm Syed Sabih ur Rehman. I'm a PhD student at United Arab Emirates University. My project is called Human CD4 CoDEG Net.

**[0:15 — Hook]**

Here is the main finding. When a human immune cell switches on to fight an infection, a set of control genes comes online. And those genes are drug targets. Many of them cause disease when they break. Genes like Zap seventy and I-T-K. I found them by mapping the full control system of a human immune cell. Let me show you how.

**[0:45 — The data | SHOW poster top]**

To do this, you need cause and effect. Not just correlation. So you switch off each gene, one at a time, and you watch what changes. The Marson lab did exactly that. They switched off every gene. In twenty two million human T cells. At rest, and after activation. For the first time, we can draw the full wiring diagram of a human immune cell.

**[1:10 — The architecture | SHOW Figure 1]**

So I drew it. The network has a very clear shape. A few genes do most of the work. The top five percent of genes control almost eighty percent of everything. Most genes do almost nothing. A theory paper predicted this shape last year, from genetics alone. My work is the first to confirm it directly.

**[1:40 — The twist | SHOW Figure 2]**

Now. What happens when the cell switches on? The shape stays the same. A few genes still control the many. That does not change. But the genes in charge change almost completely. Up to sixty percent are replaced. So the cell keeps the same shape of control. But it swaps out who is in control. The new genes in charge are the ones that read the cell's own activation signal.

**[2:10 — The payoff | SHOW Figure 5]**

Here is why this matters. These new control genes are about twice as likely to be disease genes. And they are drug targets. Zap seventy. I-T-K. L-C-K. So the control system the cell builds when it switches on is exactly where the best drug targets sit.

**[2:35 — Close | SHOW Figure 3]**

And this is not just a T cell thing. The same shape shows up in a completely different cell type. It holds up against every test I ran. And anyone can reproduce it from public data. Thank you for watching.
